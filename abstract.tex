\chapter*{Abstract}

Many of the signals handled by smart sensors are non-periodic. They stay almost constant for long intervals and change only occasionally, as a temperature reading does over time. A conventional synchronous analog-to-digital converter samples such an input at a fixed clock rate, so its comparator bank and clock distribution network keep switching and dissipating power.

This dissertation designs an asynchronous Flash ADC that removes the global clock and converts on a level-crossing basis, driven by the input itself. The dynamic power then follows the activity of the input and falls towards a static floor when the input is idle. Removing the clock also takes out the high-speed distribution network, which accounts for a large part of the power budget of a synchronous Flash, and shortens the conversion latency.

The converter is designed in a 16~nm CMOS process at 0.8~V, with 4-bit resolution and a bank of fifteen comparison slicers. A complementary five-transistor operational transconductance amplifier, used as a unity-gain voltage follower, reproduces the input across almost the whole supply range. Each slicer is a current-mirror current comparator whose decision level is set by the sizing of its transistors, so the fifteen thresholds come from sizing rather than from a reference ladder. Because those sizing-defined thresholds drift with process variation, an offline body bias trimming strategy, implemented as a Verilog-A behavioral model, adjusts the bulk voltages of each slicer to bring the trip points back across the process corners. Carried out at Synopsys Portugal, the work covers the analysis, the schematic-level design and the validation through analog co-simulation.

The converter is benchmarked against a synchronous Flash reference of the same resolution and technology, characterized under the same conditions. At a matched conversions-per-second workload, the asynchronous core draws about a ninth of the power of the reference, most of that difference being the clock distribution that the asynchronous design does not have, and the saving widens for non-periodic inputs as the converter settles towards its idle floor.

\textbf{Keywords:} Analog-to-Digital Converter, Asynchronous Design, Flash ADC, Level-Crossing Sampling, Offset Trimming, Low Power, Smart Sensors.
