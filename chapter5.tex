\section{Simulation Setup and Input Test Signals}
\label{sec:simulation_setup}

To validate the theoretical framework of Chapter~\ref{chap:async} and evaluate the physical performance of the proposed asynchronous Flash ADC, transistor-level simulations were run in a 16-nm CMOS process at a nominal supply of 0.8~V. This section describes the simulation environment, the reconstruction methodology required for non-uniform asynchronous data, and the input test stimuli.

Two families of stimuli were used. Static linearity was measured with a trapezoidal pulse spanning the effective input range of $0.1$~V to $0.7$~V (Section~\ref{subsec:5t_ota}), with equal rise and fall ramps swept from $35$~ns down to $5$~ns and a fixed $20$~ns hold between them. Dynamic performance was measured with a full-scale sinusoid at $f_{in} = 9$~MHz (amplitude $0.3$~V around a $0.4$~V common mode, again spanning $0.1$--$0.7$~V), simulated for $200$~\textmu s with transient noise enabled.

In a synchronous converter the output samples sit on a periodic clock grid. The asynchronous level-crossing Flash ADC instead produces output transitions only when a quantisation threshold is crossed, so a standard Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), which requires uniformly sampled records, cannot be applied directly to the raw output. The reconstruction used here is deliberately simple: the fifteen digital outputs and the input are linearly interpolated onto a uniform analysis grid of $180$~MHz, each interpolated output is compared against the logic threshold of $0.4$~V to recover the thermometer bits, the output code is the count of asserted bits, and the equivalent analog value follows from mapping that code back onto the $0.1$--$0.7$~V range. The analysis grid is twenty times the input frequency and coherent with it ($N = 36000$ samples place the fundamental exactly on bin $1800$), so the FFT uses a rectangular window and no spectral leakage correction is needed. The Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR), the Signal-to-Noise-and-Distortion Ratio (SINAD), the Effective Number of Bits (ENOB), the Total Harmonic Distortion (THD, first seven harmonics, aliased into the first Nyquist zone where necessary) and the Spurious-Free Dynamic Range (SFDR) are extracted from this spectrum.

The same resampled record defines the bubble error rate used throughout this chapter: a sample exhibits a bubble error when its fifteen-bit thermometer pattern contains more than one $0\leftrightarrow 1$ transition, and the bubble rate is the fraction of analysis samples for which this happens.

The choice of $9$~MHz is tied to the hysteresis analysis of Section~\ref{subsec:static_ramp}. A full-scale sinusoid at $f_{in}$ has a peak slew rate of $2\pi f_{in} A$, which for $9$~MHz and $0.3$~V of amplitude gives $\approx 17.0$~V/\textmu s, the same slew rate as the fastest ramp that meets the $1$~LSB hysteresis criterion ($0.6$~V$/35$~ns $\approx 17.1$~V/\textmu s). The sinusoid therefore never exceeds the tracking envelope validated by the ramp test, and the hysteresis observed at faster ramps does not affect the dynamic measurement; the absence of bubble errors over the whole record confirms it. By the same criterion, a full-scale sinusoid at $20$~MHz would reach $37.7$~V/\textmu s and exceed the envelope, so $9$~MHz is, in practice, the full-scale sinusoidal bandwidth of the converter.

Table~\ref{tab:dynamic_results} summarises the dynamic metrics extracted from the $9$~MHz transient noise test. The SINAD of $26.50$~dB corresponds to an ENOB of $4.11$~bits, marginally above the nominal 4-bit resolution; this is consistent with the level-crossing property discussed in Chapter~\ref{chap:lit_review}, whereby the absence of a uniform sampling clock removes the conventional quantisation noise floor and can push the SNDR past the limit of a clocked converter with the same nominal resolution. No bubble errors were observed over the $36000$ analysis samples of this test.

\section{Static Linearity Results}
\label{sec:static_linearity}

The static linearity metrics, Differential Non-Linearity (DNL) and Integral Non-Linearity (INL), quantify the deterministic mismatches of the architecture and the circuit. The proposed asynchronous Flash ADC contains no reference ladder, so its DNL and INL are set by the deviations of the slicer trip points from their nominal sizing-defined positions: the deterministic, corner-dependent shifts analysed in Chapter~\ref{chap:trimming}, plus the random threshold variations of the slicer bank.

\subsection{Threshold Extraction and Linearity Versus Conversion Rate}
\label{subsec:static_ramp}

The fifteen decision thresholds were measured with the trapezoidal pulse described in Section~\ref{sec:simulation_setup}. For each slicer, the input voltage at which its digital output crosses the $0.4$~V logic threshold is recorded by linear interpolation, once on the rising ramp and once on the falling ramp. The difference between the two readings is the input-referred hysteresis of that slicer; their average is taken as its decision threshold. The DNL of each of the sixteen codes follows from the widths between consecutive sorted thresholds relative to the ideal LSB of $37.5$~mV, the INL of each transition from the deviation of its threshold from the ideal uniform level, and the offset from the mean of those deviations. Since the widths of the two end codes depend directly on the chosen range limits, the DNL figures reported below exclude them and cover codes 1 to 14.

The hysteresis is not a designed property of the slicers: it is the input-referred signature of the conversion delay $\tau_{conv}$ modelled in Section~\ref{subsec:slope_overload}. While the input ramps, every delay in the follower and the slicer translates into a voltage error proportional to the ramp rate, so the rising and falling readings separate as the ramp gets steeper. A measurement is considered valid when the worst-case hysteresis stays below $1$~LSB. Table~\ref{tab:static_ramp} reports the static metrics for ramp times from $35$~ns down to $5$~ns, with the equivalent conversion rate computed from the active ramp time (and, in parentheses in the discussion below, with the $20$~ns hold included).

The correction is effective but not uniform across corners. \section{Random Mismatch: Monte Carlo Analysis}
\label{sec:mc_mismatch}

The corner-indexed trimming of Chapter~\ref{chap:trimming} corrects deterministic shifts only; random intra-die mismatch remains uncorrected by design. To quantify how much linearity that limitation costs, a 330-run Monte Carlo simulation was performed on two adjacent slicer outputs, out$_0$ and out$_1$. In each run, the decision threshold of each output was extracted as the input voltage at which the output crosses the $0.4$~V logic level, and the width of the code between them follows from the difference of the two thresholds; the DNL of that code is the deviation of this width from the ideal LSB. \section{Power Dissipation and Energy Efficiency}
\label{sec:power_efficiency}

A defining feature of the asynchronous Flash ADC is its signal-dependent power profile. In direct contrast to traditional synchronous architectures that exhibit a static power baseline due to continuous clock toggling, the dynamic power of the proposed converter is governed strictly by the frequency of threshold-crossing events.

\subsection{Power Measurement Methodology}
\label{sec:power_methodology}

To keep the comparison between the two converters fair, the power figures reported here cover only the core conversion path of each design. The encoder, that is, the digital logic that translates the thermometer code into the final binary word, was excluded from all power measurements on both sides of the comparison.

The reasoning is straightforward: both designs require some form of thermometer-to-binary conversion at their outputs, and this encoding logic is largely independent of whether the preceding conversion is synchronous or asynchronous. Including it on one side of the comparison but not the other would artificially inflate one power budget and lead to a conclusion that does not fairly reflect the underlying architectural trade-off. The most defensible approach is therefore to exclude it from both and compare only the converter cores.

Under this methodology, the power reported for the synchronous reference in Table~\ref{tab:sync_results} includes the resistor ladder, the 15 StrongARM latch comparators, the ring oscillator and the tapered clock buffer chain described in Chapter~\ref{chap:synchronous}. The clock distribution path is precisely the overhead that the asynchronous design eliminates. The power reported for the proposed asynchronous converter includes the voltage follower, the slicer bank and the event-generation logic of Chapter~\ref{chap:async}.

\subsection{Signal-Dependent Power Scaling}

Two operating points were measured directly. With the input active, the converter draws $181$~\textmu W; with the input idle, it draws $145$~\textmu W. The idle figure is the static floor anticipated in Section~\ref{subsec:resolution_power}: because the complementary follower operates within the $0.1$--$0.7$~V window and the input never reaches the bottom rail, the input stages conduct a residual bias current even when nothing is being converted. The difference between the two measurements, $36$~\textmu W, is the activity-dependent component of the consumption. Figure~\ref{fig:power_scaling} shows the dependency of the total power on the input frequency.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    % \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{figures/power_scaling.pdf}
    \vspace{5cm} % Placeholder space for the image
    \caption{Total and dynamic power consumption as a function of the input signal frequency ($f_{in}$).}

Energy efficiency in this non-uniform context is assessed with the adapted Walden Figure of Merit ($FoM_{W,adapted}$) introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:lit_review}, which uses the effective average sampling rate ($f_{s,avg}$) set by the crossing density and therefore allows a fair benchmark against synchronous designs:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{FoM}_{W,adapted} = \frac{P_{total}}{2^{\mathrm{ENOB}} \cdot f_{s,avg}}
\end{equation}

In this work, $f_{s,avg}$ is obtained directly from the simulation record: the conversion events generated over the observation window are counted and divided by the window duration. Table~\ref{tab:state_of_the_art} reports each figure of merit next to the $f_{s,avg}$ used to compute it.

\section{Synchronous Reference Flash ADC Results}
\label{sec:sync_results}

The synchronous reference converter described in Chapter~\ref{chap:synchronous} was simulated under the same 16-nm, $0.8$~V conditions as the proposed design, following the power measurement methodology of Section~\ref{sec:power_methodology}. Its static linearity was characterised with a $20$~MHz input sampled at $640$~MS/s over the same $0.1$--$0.7$~V range. The power was measured at the workload-matched operating point defined below, a $9$~MHz full-scale input sampled at $288$~MS/s, and the consumption came out the same, $727$~\textmu W, at both clock rates.

Neither operating point comes from the original publication. The design of \cite{flashadc_ref} was demonstrated at $20$~MS/s with a $332$~kHz input in 65~nm; reproducing that point here would compare the two converters in a regime unrelated to the one the asynchronous design targets. The matched point follows instead from the activity model of Section~\ref{subsec:resolution_power} and from the slew rate envelope of Section~\ref{sec:simulation_setup}: the fastest full-scale sinusoid the asynchronous converter tracks cleanly is $9$~MHz, and at that input a 4-bit level-crossing converter generates $f_{s,avg} = 2^{N+1} f_{in} = 32 \times 9~\mathrm{MHz} = 288$~M crossings per second. Clocked at $288$~MS/s with the same $9$~MHz stimulus, the synchronous reference performs the same number of conversions per second, on the same input, as the asynchronous converter in the test of Section~\ref{sec:simulation_setup}. The power figures of the two converters therefore describe the same workload. Its static linearity is good: the worst-case DNL and INL both stay within approximately $0.27$~LSB, and the transfer function shows all sixteen codes with no missing levels (Figure~\ref{fig:sync_linearity}). The performance summary is presented in Table~\ref{tab:sync_results} and serves as the baseline for the comparative analysis of the following section.

The comparison makes the trade-off concrete. On the same $9$~MHz full-scale stimulus and at a matched conversions-per-second workload (Section~\ref{sec:sync_results}), the complete synchronous converter draws $727$~\textmu W against $181$~\textmu W for the active asynchronous core, a factor of four. A substantial part of the synchronous budget goes to clock generation and distribution, blocks that do not exist in the asynchronous design. The synchronous baseline keeps the simpler control block and the higher conversion rate, but its power envelope is rigid and its efficiency collapses when the input is barely active. The asynchronous implementation scales its energy with the input activity and reaches a competitive Walden Figure of Merit, particularly once the body bias trimming network restores the effective resolution under process variations.

A note on the fairness of this comparison is warranted, since an activity-indexed figure of merit could be suspected of favouring the asynchronous design by construction. Three observations bound that concern. First, both converters have the same 4-bit resolution and their cores are measured under the same exclusions (Section~\ref{sec:power_methodology}), so the structural terms of the comparison are symmetric. Second, the advantage predicted by the activity-dependent model is conditional, and the condition is stated by the model itself (Section~\ref{subsec:resolution_power}): as input activity rises, $f_{s,avg}$ approaches $2^{N+1} f_{in}$ and the dynamic power of the asynchronous core grows linearly, until near the slope-overload limit the activity advantage disappears altogether. The asynchronous architecture is expected to win for sparse inputs and to lose its margin for continuously active inputs near full bandwidth; this comparison targets the former regime because that is the application space defined in Chapter~\ref{chap:intro}. Third, the one structural asymmetry that remains, the restricted effective input range of the complementary follower, runs against the asynchronous design rather than in its favour.
